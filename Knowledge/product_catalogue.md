Dear partner, dear customer,
You are now holding the Synguard 'Access Managementí catalogue. It is almost two hundred pages, as our product range has expanded considerably over the years. We are happy to share all our know-how with you, to ensure that you can find the most suitable access management solution.
As an early adopter of the latest trends in the market, we know a lot about the increasing importance of security, the shift to the cloud, the growing role of smartphones as access providers and the demand to connect different security solutions. It is all in this guide. 
Access control is no longer just a technical issue. Access management is more than just physical security: it improves access flows in an organisation and increases efficiency, security and user experience.
This catalogue aims to guide you step by step through our access management solutions:
1. What are in fact the strong and unique features of our solutions? In Chapters 2, 3 and 4 youíll find an overview.
2. What is the current situation? Is it a new building or an existing building? Are there multiple sites? Do you want to go for a cloud or an on-premises solution? In Chapter 5 you can read which set-up you can offer for each possibility. 
3. How do they want visitors to identify themselves? With a traditional badge? Via facial recognition? Via smartphone? A combination of different identification options? Chapter 6 will discuss this in more detail.
4. Now that you know what the customer wants, choose the most suitable hardware: controllers, readers, locksÖ This is explained in Chapter 7 .
5. Then choose the necessary software modules. All information can be found in Chapter 8.
6. Synguard also has specific solutions for customers with specific requirements. The most important ones are listed in Chapter 9. 
7. The responsibility to support the software and keep it up to date depends on the type of solution (on-premise versus SaaS) To know what to expect from this service, go to Chapter 10.
We hope that all this information will help you to present the best possible solution to each of your customers. If you have any other questions, please do not hesitate to contact us. We are here for you - "ALWAYS WITH YOU"
Bert Meert

Managing Director Synguard

4-5: The difference between a configured reader and a reader in transparent mode

In our new SynConEvo controller, we have further enhanced the security by including a Secure Access Module (or SAM). Thanks to a SAM, readers can work in transparent mode. This means that the security keys for reading badges are no longer programmed reader by reader,  but are only contained in the door controller. In this way, a customer can fully own their own encryption. The installer or manufacturer no longer gets to see the encryption keys. An additional advantage is that the DESFire keys are no longer attached to the unsecured side of the door (in the badge readers), but in the protected zone where the door controller is also located.
This 'transparent mode' of the badge readers allows the badge to communicate directly with the door controller, both of which contain the necessary DESFire keys. 
Between the door controller and the IP controller AES encryption keys are used. This means that the door controller and IP master controller can only communicate with each other if they have the same 128-bit AES encryption key. AES stands for 'Advanced Encryption Standardí and has been included in the ISO/IEC 18033-3 standard since 2002. 

Both between IP controller and server and between IP controller and user interface a TLS certificate is used. This stands for 'Transport Layer Securityí and provides a secure layer between the IP controller and the server. A TLS certificate uses the https protocol of the browser chosen by the user to consult the Synguard solution. The browser establishes a secure connection via port 443 by default.

5.2.3 Online vs offline
Online or offline, that's the choice between real-time monitoring of access control or periodic updates of events and accesses. 
Online wireless locks transmit changes to the access control system in real time. A new employee can therefore use his badge on all locks immediately. 
The wireless lock communicates via a hub or gateway that is suspended close to the lock (generally up to Ī25 m) and connected to our IP controller.
When 	an 	employee 	presents 	his 	badge 	to 	the wireless online lock, the lock will transmit the data to the hub. The connection to the IP controller checks whether that person has permission to pass the barrier at this time. So, the IP controller makes the access authorisation  decision. If the person is authorised to access, the lock is controlled to open via the hub.

5 3: Schematic representation of a wireless online set-upBecause there is a constant connection between lock, hub and IP controller, all events are live. The access control screen will therefore also show who is requesting access and whether this is allowed.

Offline wireless locks require wall readers (updaters) to write or update the employee's profile and access rights on  the badge. An offline wireless system is therefore also often called a data-on-card system.
The period of validity of the assigned rights can be configured in the access control system. This ensures that no one can book Ďindefinitelyí without passing through the wall terminal again. The wall terminal is a read/write reader. This means that the reader can write all kinds of data to the card (such as access rights), but can also read the card again. This keeps track of who booked where and when.  
If an employee presents his badge to the wireless offline lock, the lock will check if the badge has the appropriate access rights. The lock then writes some data back to the card, such as the date, time and whether or not access was obtained.
When the employee presents his badge again to the wall terminal, the wall terminal will read the data and transmit it to the IP controller. At that moment, the events of that person become visible in the access control system. 
Since a lot of data is written by both updater and locks, it is recommended to use a badge with at least 4KB memory for offline systems. The wall terminal can also be used to control a wired door and to update cards.
The wall readers are preferably IP readers (SynRea-IDMAX50), these are capable of fast read/write performance. 
Certain RS485 readers can also have an R/W function, e.g. STid readers.  When this happens over RS485 (OSDP or SSCP), you need to consider a longer read/write time (read: hold badge in front of the reader) More information on this can be found in 7.6.1 Wired badge readers.

6.2	Biometrics
Biometrics refers to all solutions that make use of measurable characteristics of persons, such as fingerprints. Thanks to these unique, personal characteristics, a person's identity can be established. 
One of the advantages is that, unlike a classic badge, you can never forget  it.  Another plus is that no one can borrow it, so you can always be sure who gets access. Of course, a biometric feature must  always be registered in the system  for a certain person before it can be used to grant access. Synguard has integrated fingerprint, palm vein and facial scans into its access management system.
Because biometric (personal, medical) data are preferably not stored in a database (GDPR), some devices use 'template on card'. This means that the biometric data is stored on a badge. So the holder of the badge is also the holder of his own biometric data, without these being stored in a database. To apply this method, you need an R/W reader near the biometric reader. It first reads the template on the card and compares it with thetemplate offered on the biometric reader.
Template on card is possible with the Palmki palm reader, among others, in combination with a SynReg reader. 
Biometric options are:
ē Fingerprint 
ē Facial recognition 
ē Palm vein 

6.2.1	Fingerprint recognition
Of the various biometric solutions, this technology is the most frequently used today. An employee places his finger - previously registered in the access management system - on the reader and is thus granted access to the closed area or not. 
6.2.2	Palm vein recognition
This technology compares the vein pattern of the hand you present with what is in the system. More than five million reference points of the vein pattern are captured by a high-precision infrared sensor. 
If the vein pattern matches, access is granted. The difference with fingerprint technology is that it does not use a surface pattern, but scans the veins in the hand for more reference points and then compares them. No need to touch the scanning device in this case.
6.2.3	Facial recognition
Of the 3 biometric solutions offered by Synguard, this is the one that most appeals to the imagination. The facial markers of the person in front of the camera are compared to those in the system. It is important to choose a camera that has live-face detection, i.e. a camera that checks whether there is a person in front of the camera and not a photograph. 

6.3 Smartphone
This means using a smartphone to open doors via Bluetooth and/or NFC. The use of a QR code on a mobile phone is explained in Chapter 6.4 QR.

Examples of these brands are HID, Idesco, SimonsVoss, STid and Aperio from Assa Abloy. As a result, there are many possibilities for mobile applications in an access control system.
For access control identification by smartphone (NFC and/ or BLE), mobile credentials are always required. These can be purchased or leased or be free of charge. They always work in combination with a free app on your smartphone. Each credential can be seen as a software badge that allows you to identify someone. 
The communication between the smartphone and the readers runs via Bluetooth Low Energy and/or NFC. If a reader can only use NFC, remember that there are currently no apps on Apple products that use NFC.
BLE, on the other hand, is available on all popular operating systems. The most important matter is to ensure that the chosen system works with the various operating systems used on mobile devices within the company (Android, Apple...). All readers offered by Synguard make use of both NFC and BLE.

6.6 Long range tags
Long range or Electronic Product Code (EPC) tags work in the same way as ordinary badges, but give a user access from a greater distance. To this end they use UHF or Ultra High Frequency. This means that the frequency at which the tag and reader operate is between 860 MHz and 960 MHz. 
This technology is used when cars need to be able to enter a garage without holding a badge against a reader, or when a certain gate needs to open automatically when a forklift truck is nearby. 
The reading distance of the tags is up to 10 m. This also requires special UHF readers, which are described in Chapter 7.6.7 Long range readers.
Depending on the application, there are different types of tags. For example, there are tags that are specially made to be placed on the windscreen of a vehicle. 
They cannot be moved to another vehicle and they break down when an attempt is made to remove them. Other tags are made specifically to be used on metal, as in the case of a forklift truck.


6.7	Electronic keys
Electronic keys are also known as intelligent or smart keys, because the electronics contained in the key determine whether a user is allowed access to the door that they want to open. 
The key also contains a battery. Therefore, no battery is required in the cylinder or padlock. This makes the use of electronic keys especially interesting in places where no power, cabling or network is available. This might be in the case of remote fences or where a digital cylinder is the most appropriate locking device, such as footpath boxes or switchboards. Because the battery is in the key and not in the cylinder, maintenance is easy.

7.5.5	Magnet contacts
Magnetic contacts are designed to know the position of the door or gate.
A magnetic contact consists of 2 parts:
ē The wired part that is mounted on/in the fixed door frame 
ē The loose magnet that is mounted on/in the moving part
When the magnet comes close enough to the reed contact, the door position will be transmitted to the access control. Magnetic contacts are connected to an input of the door controller. In this way, the access control system knows the status of the door or gate and can adjust its operation depending on the door position. For example, an alarm can be triggered if the door is left open too long or when it is forced (opening without valid action).
The status change of the door or gate can be displayed on a map or an action can be linked to it, such as triggering an alarm.
Magnet contacts are available in different variants to offer a solution for any door or barrier:
ē Embedded magnetic contacts
ē Surface-mounted magnetic contacts

7.5.7	Emergency push buttons
Green emergency push buttons are used to unlock a door under access control in case of emergency. They must always physically interrupt the voltage of the fail-safe lock directly, so that the lock is released at all times when that button is pressed.
Emergency buttons should always be placed in series between the door controller and the fail-safe lock. If the emergency button and the lock were wired separately (in star), there are 2 disadvantages:
ē The distance of the lock voltage doubles: the lock voltage first passes over the emergency button, back to the controller and only then to the lock.
ē If the emergency button cable melts (or is damaged in any way) in a fire situation, causing the conductors to connect to each other, the emergency button no longer works. This problem cannot occur when the emergency button is wired in series with the lock. If the cable is damaged in this case, a short circuit occurs and the fail safe lock is released.We recommend using emergency buttons that have multiple contacts available. Thus, in addition to the interruption of the lock voltage, a feedback to the access control system can also be carried out. For example, an alarm can be generated in the software to indicate that a door has been unlocked in an emergency. 
For best feedback & indication, we recommend an emergency button with built-in LED lighting (red-green) and buzzer, which also indicate locally that the button has been used.

7.7.2	Handle-operated or solenoid locks
In the case of solenoid locks, handle control is released by means of a coil (solenoid). However, the lock is still opened manually, using hand power. The lock itself is mechanically interlocked on both the latch and dead bolt and ensures that the door is always locked when closed. In the case of a multi-point lock, it is also locked on both security locks. Locks can always be opened mechanically by means of a cylinder and they can control single-sided and double-sided cranks. 
These locks can be used on internal and external doors. They are installed in the door leaf and must therefore also be wired into the door leaf. The transition between the door frame and the door leaf can be made with a built-in or surface-mounted cable gland. 
The solenoid locks run on 12VDC-24VDC and can be set according to the fail safe- or fail secure-
principle. A feedback contact is available via a mechanical 7-55: Solenoid lock push button.

7.7.3	Door magnets
Door magnets (also called magnetic locks) use electromagnetism to close a door. A door magnet consists of 2 components: a  housing with the magnet itself that should be  connected to a power supply and the counter plate (also called anchor plate). When the magnet is energised, it attracts the armature plate, which then magnetically sticks to the magnet. 
When the electromagnet is not energised, it releases the counter plate and the door can be opened. A door magnet is therefore always fail safe.
Door magnets can be fitted on both inward and outward swinging doors. Depending on the direction of rotation, the appropriate mounting brackets must be provided. Most magnetic locks are available with a signal contact that indicates whether the magnet is holding its counter plate (= door fixed). The holding power of door magnets is expressed in kg or Newton (1 kg = 10 N). Door magnets with a holding power of 250 to 600 kg are commonly used. Magnets operate on 12VDC-24VDC. This solution is most commonly used for access control on existing doors or at gates/fencing.
Door magnets are also available in more variants, such as embedded models, watertight models or models incorporated into a strip with a handle that can be mounted over the entire height of the door.

7.7.4	Motor locks
The motor lock is opened by means of a motor, whereby the dead bolt is withdrawn and the latch bolt released. When closing the door, the lock mechanically locks on both the latch and the deadbolt. The lock also has a very useful self-correcting effect in case of tampering. The dead bolt will always be retracted first and only be withdrawn when the door falls into the rebate. Locking is therefore guaranteed.
Motor locks are available in different sizes. The installation of a motor lock in a door unit is very specific work, we recommend it to be carried out by a  specialised locksmith.
Moreover, they are mainly used in exterior doors. The lock is installed in the door leaf and therefore has to be wired up to there as well. The transition between the door frame and the door leaf can be made with a built-in or surface mounted cable gland. 
7.7.5	Electric bolts
Drop bolts or electromechanical bolts are locks for higher security applications. With a  closing force of up to 2,500 kg, they are the right choice for professional security applications, such as the security doors of banks, currency exchange offices,  jewellery shops, police departments and prisons. In addition, they can also be the right choice to solve specific existing situations with an embedded lock. 
The door is locked automatically  when the electronic contact in the latch and the magnetic part in the strike come into contact. The magnetic part is adjustable, so that the switching moment of the latch can be accurately adjusted. Due to this closing method and the potential-free contact on the blocked bolt, the signal 'closed and locked' is shown. The latch is standard key-operated to mechanically override the latch.
Assa Abloy motor locks operate on 12VDC-24VDC and according to the fail-safe principle. A feedback contact is available. Finally, a motor lock must always be fitted with a corresponding control module.

Once a software link has been established, harmonious cooperation between access management and lift control is possible management and lift control possible. A surgeon, for example, has the highest priority and naturally takes precedence over an administrative assistant. For example, an emergency worker can call all lifts together with high priority.
